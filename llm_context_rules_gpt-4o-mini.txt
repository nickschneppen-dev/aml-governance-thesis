[Rule 1]: Prioritize qualitative context over quantitative flags when assessing risk.
Applies when: High fan-in or fan-out metrics are present, but qualitative evidence indicates legitimate operations (e.g., registered charities or compliant businesses).
Rationale: Many incorrect cases involved agents over-weighting quantitative metrics like fan-in or fan-out without adequately considering the qualitative context that indicated legitimate operations, leading to false positives.

[Rule 2]: Adjust risk assessments based on the nature of the business model.
Applies when: The business model is cash-intensive or operates in a heavily regulated sector (e.g., payroll, charity).
Rationale: Agents often failed to recognize that legitimate cash-intensive businesses can exhibit high transaction volumes without indicating illicit activity, resulting in false positives.

[Rule 3]: Emphasize the importance of finding an "alibi" in the knowledge base.
Applies when: High-risk flags are triggered by transaction metrics, but there is supporting qualitative intelligence that suggests legitimacy.
Rationale: Agents frequently overlooked the need to find mitigating factors in the knowledge base that could justify downgrading risk scores, leading to incorrect assessments.

[Rule 4]: Balance conflicting evidence carefully.
Applies when: There are both high-risk indicators and qualitative evidence suggesting legitimacy.
Rationale: In many cases, agents did not adequately weigh conflicting evidence, leading to either overestimating or underestimating risk. A balanced approach is crucial for accurate assessments.

[Rule 5]: Recognize the implications of scrutiny from regulatory bodies.
Applies when: There is evidence of increased scrutiny or regulatory oversight in the industry related to the client.
Rationale: Agents often failed to adjust risk scores appropriately when regulatory scrutiny was mentioned, which could indicate a higher risk environment.

[Rule 6]: Consider the total transaction volume in relation to average transaction amounts.
Applies when: The average transaction amount is high, but the total volume is low or moderate.
Rationale: Agents sometimes misjudged the risk by focusing solely on high average transaction amounts without considering the total volume, which could indicate legitimate business activity.

[Rule 7]: Identify and address systemic risks in the industry.
Applies when: Qualitative intelligence indicates broader industry issues that could affect the client's legitimacy.
Rationale: Agents often did not adequately consider systemic risks highlighted in qualitative articles, which could elevate the risk profile of clients operating in vulnerable sectors.

[Rule 8]: Use caution with high fan-out metrics.
Applies when: A client has a high fan-out but operates in a legitimate context (e.g., payroll).
Rationale: Many false positives were attributed to high fan-out metrics without recognizing that legitimate businesses can have many recipients due to their operational nature.

[Rule 9]: Override clean quantitative data with qualitative evidence when necessary.
Applies when: Qualitative intelligence indicates potential illicit activity despite clean quantitative metrics.
Rationale: Agents often failed to flag cases correctly when qualitative evidence suggested risk, leading to false negatives.

[Rule 10]: Ensure that the implications of adverse intelligence are fully integrated into risk assessments.
Applies when: There is adverse intelligence in the knowledge base that could indicate potential wrongdoing.
Rationale: Agents frequently did not give enough weight to adverse intelligence, leading to underestimating risk in cases where qualitative evidence suggested potential issues.
