[Rule 1]: When a single structural flag is fully explained by a strong business‑model “alibi,” down‑weight the flag sharply.  
Applies when:  
- Only one of the core quantitative heuristics is triggered (e.g., HIGH FAN-IN, HIGH FAN-OUT, or HIGH AVG TRANSACTION),  
- And there is high‑quality qualitative evidence (regulator, government, reputable media) that this exact structural pattern is normal for the client’s business type (e.g., charities with many donors, payroll-heavy firms with many payees, cash businesses with large individual deposits).  
Rationale:  
- In nearly all false‑positive traps (charity, payroll, structurer, high‑roller groups), the agent correctly found a sector‑specific explanation and reduced risk.  
- In the incorrect case, the agent recognised that an auction house could explain high fan-out but still kept the risk elevated instead of allowing the benign explanation to dominate.  
- Future reasoning should treat a well‑supported business‑model alibi as the default interpretation, unless there are additional independent red flags (adverse news, multiple metrics triggered, high‑risk jurisdictions, etc.).

---

[Rule 2]: Do not treat “inherent sector risk” or “proposed regulation” as a stand‑alone reason to push a clean, well‑explained profile above low risk.  
Applies when:  
- Qualitative sources speak about sector‑wide vulnerabilities or proposed AML coverage (e.g., art dealers, lawyers, event managers, luxury hotels, casinos),  
- But there is no entity‑specific adverse media or regulatory action, and  
- The client’s transaction metrics are moderate and structurally well‑explained by the business model.  
Rationale:  
- In many correct cases (law firm trust accounts, mining, events, hotels, parking, auction‑like high‑roller clients) the agent allowed sector risk to keep scores at medium, even when entity‑specific data were clean.  
- The auction‑house case that was mis‑classified shows over‑weighting of “sector vulnerability” and under‑weighting of (a) moderate volumes, (b) single explained flag, (c) lack of any adverse intel.  
- Sector risk should adjust the baseline slightly (e.g., 0→low or very low‑medium), not drive a materially higher rating when everything else looks benign.

---

[Rule 3]: When volumes and averages are clearly sub‑threshold and only one graph metric is abnormal, consider whether you are seeing only a partial view of a legitimate high‑turnover business before inferring laundering.  
Applies when:  
- Total volume is below the elevated‑throughput benchmark (~$200k in this setup),  
- Average amount is below $1,000,  
- Fan-in is low or normal, and  
- Only fan-out or fan-in is modestly above threshold (80/20), with no other anomalies.  
Rationale:  
- In many high‑roller traps (fintech IPO, lithium miner, luxury imports, casino, hospital, mining, law firms), the system saw “big business, small slice” – the on‑us account carried only a small operational subset relative to public revenues.  
- The incorrect auction‑house case had exactly this pattern (moderate volume, low average, single high fan-out), yet the agent treated it as meaningfully elevated risk rather than as one operating account in a larger, legitimate business.  
- Recognising “partial window into a larger legitimate operation” avoids inflating risk ratings where nothing suggests the slice itself is being abused.

---

[Rule 4]: For charities and NGOs, treat high fan-in (and moderate fan-out) as business‑normal when supported by registration and regulator oversight, and default to low risk unless there is entity‑specific negative intel.  
Applies when:  
- HIGH FAN-IN (>20) is present, with modest volumes and average amounts,  
- The knowledge base indicates the entity is a registered charity/NGO (ACNC or equivalent) with current registration and/or audited accounts,  
- There is no specific allegation of misuse of funds, extremism, or terrorism links.  
Rationale:  
- Across many charity traps, the agent correctly matched “many donors, many beneficiaries” to donation‑funded NGOs and still often left scores in medium.  
- The training signal: these are canonical false positives; registration plus positive/neutral regulator commentary is strong evidence to downgrade to low.  
- Only sector‑level comments (“charities can be abused”) appeared, and those did not correlate with actual ML in any trap; they should not be enough to hold the rating in medium absent specific concerns (e.g., related‑party abuse escalating, terror designations, sanctions).

---

[Rule 5]: For payroll / labour‑intensive entities, interpret very high fan‑out as benign when aligned with documented headcount and payroll/contractor models.  
Applies when:  
- HIGH FAN-OUT (>80) is observed,  
- Fan-in is low/moderate, volume and average amount are mid‑range, and  
- Qualitative evidence clearly describes a business with many employees/contractors (security firms, cleaning companies, construction, staffing agencies, retailers, SaaS/startups, gig/freelance platforms, property managers, distributors).  
Rationale:  
- In payroll traps, agent performance was consistently correct: they looked for explicit evidence of large workforces, contracts, or platform payouts and then de‑emphasised the fan‑out flag.  
- These same patterns should lead to an “often low” classification, not consistently medium, once the payroll explanation is well supported and there is no other ML signal.  
- High fan‑out, in isolation, is a weak ML indicator in these contexts and should not dominate over a strong payroll narrative.

---

[Rule 6]: For “structurer”‑type flags (high average transaction value), rely heavily on sector‑consistent pricing and billing norms before inferring laundering.  
Applies when:  
- HIGH AVG TRANSACTION (> $1,000) is the main or only flag,  
- Volume is modest (<$200k) and fan‑in/fan‑out are unremarkable, and  
- Articles describe sectors where per‑transaction values are naturally high: medical specialists, cosmetic clinics, used‑car dealers, import–export traders, luxury hotels, tattoo studios, electricians, vending operators, parking, small contractors.  
Rationale:  
- Correct cases repeatedly show that high avg size is fully explained by ordinary pricing (e.g., $900–1,100 Medicare rebates, $1k+ car/tattoo/electrical invoices, trade shipments, luxury rooms).  
- The agent still tended to keep risk in medium in many of these – the training labels say these are false positives.  
- Going forward, when sector pricing directly matches the numeric pattern and no other strong risk factors exist, high‑avg flags should usually be discounted and risk pushed towards low.

---

[Rule 7]: When credible law‑enforcement or FIU/intelligence sources allege specific financial‑crime typologies, override “clean” transaction data.  
Applies when:  
- Qualitative sources include AFP, AUSTRAC, DFAT, FIUs, or similar,  
- They allege concrete roles (financial controller, facilitator, hawala operator, proliferation financier, sanctions evader, human‑trafficking facilitator, ransomware laundering node, tax‑evasion facilitator), and  
- The account’s metrics are modest and may be intentionally engineered to avoid detection.  
Rationale:  
- In all sleeper/false‑negative traps, the only real signal was high‑quality adverse intel; metrics were deliberately mundane. The agent correctly rated these high risk by giving decisive weight to the intel.  
- In this class of scenarios, benign‑looking volume, fan‑in/fan‑out, and averages are not mitigating – they are part of the typology (small, routine flows).  
- Systematic rule: law‑enforcement/FIU‑grade adverse intel trumps a lack of quantitative flags unless the intel is clearly weak, outdated, or rebutted.

---

[Rule 8]: Treat favourable “human interest” or reputational articles as very weak evidence against specific adverse intel.  
Applies when:  
- One article portrays the subject as community‑minded, diligent, successful, charitable, etc.,  
- Another article or official source alleges direct involvement in serious crime (drug networks, trafficking, bribery, sanctions evasion, ransomware, hawala), and  
- Transaction data are compatible with either story.  
Rationale:  
- In multiple sleeper cases (refugee small business + people‑smuggling facilitation; mechanic + drug controller; volunteer mentor + trafficking facilitator; pro‑bono lawyer + bribery conduit; retiree + sanctions/proliferation activity), positive character coverage co‑existed with heavy adverse intel.  
- The correct decisions always weighted law‑enforcement and detailed investigative reporting far more than positive local press; this is realistic: offenders often have good surface reputations.  
- Future logic should never “average out” good‑news and bad‑news articles; bad‑news from credible authorities should be decisive.

---

[Rule 9]: When adverse media describes a typology that explicitly explains the otherwise “normal” metrics, interpret the absence of system flags as confirmatory of the typology, not as mitigation.  
Applies when:  
- Articles from credible sources state that the subject kept transactions “small,” “routine,” “unremarkable,” or “below thresholds” to evade detection,  
- Quantitative metrics indeed show modest volumes, sub‑threshold averages, and no automated flags, and  
- The typology involves covert use of personal or business accounts (bribery, sanctions evasion, trafficking, ransomware, hawala, informal value transfer).  
Rationale:  
- In many sleeper cases, investigators explicitly said the account looked like normal income or small flows, and that this was intentional. The agent correctly matched those descriptions to the quiet numeric profile and raised risk to high.  
- The mis‑taken instinct in many AML systems is “no flags → lower risk”; the rule must invert that when the intelligence *predicted* that no flags would fire.  
- Seeing the exact pattern investigators highlight (small, routine flows) should increase confidence in the adverse story, not comfort.

---

[Rule 10]: For sector‑wide compliance or governance concerns unrelated to ML/TF, keep them as a weak, secondary factor.  
Applies when:  
- Articles discuss audits, Fair Work reviews, wage underpayment probes, tax/GST compliance sweeps, governance reviews of charities, environmental/regulatory crackdowns, without naming specific misconduct by the client; and  
- No AML‑relevant issues (fraud, corruption, sanctions, TF, organised crime) are alleged.  
Rationale:  
- In many correct cases, these sectoral stories (Fair Work, ATO, ACNC, labour, environmental, GST crackdowns) existed but did not correlate with any ML behaviour in the labelled data.  
- The agent often let these concerns push everything to medium; the training labels signal that this is too conservative.  
- Such stories should only slightly nudge risk upward when everything else is clean and well‑explained, not be used to justify medium‑plus scores on their own.

---

[Rule 11]: Use “multiple independent anomalies” as the bar for medium/high when there is no adverse intel; a single explained anomaly should normally map to low.  
Applies when:  
- Only one of the key quantitative risk flags is triggered, and that flag has a strong, documented business‑model explanation,  
- KYC/news show a regulated, established entity with oversight (licences, ACNC registration, ASIC filings, audits), and  
- There is no law‑enforcement or sanctions adverse media.  
Rationale:  
- Reviewing the training set, truly suspicious cases generally involved either adverse intel or a constellation of issues; benign trap cases almost always had exactly one mechanical flag that was fully explained (charity fan‑in, payroll fan‑out, structurer avg).  
- The incorrect auction‑house decision illustrates over‑penalising a single explained high fan‑out plus generic sector risk.  
- A more calibrated rule: reserve medium/high for (a) adverse intel, or (b) combinations of unexplained metrics (e.g., high fan‑in + high fan‑out + near‑threshold volume) or inconsistent narratives.

---

[Rule 12]: Always test for “business-structure coherence” before inferring ML from graph structure.  
Applies when:  
- You see high fan‑out or fan‑in, but also have rich qualitative detail about the entity’s operations (headcount, number of donors/clients, number of outlets, scale of contracts), and  
- The observed numbers (count of senders/recipients, ticket sizes, total volumes) are consistent with that operational description.  
Rationale:  
- In most correct cases, the agent implicitly performed this coherence test (e.g., “95 recipients ≈ 90–200 staff”, “22 senders vs 3,000 donors = actually small slice”, “avg $1,040 matches ticket pricing”).  
- Codifying that test formalises the idea: if the numbers line up with the stated scale and structure, the graph pattern is likely business‑normal rather than ML.  
- In the auction case, the agent focused on the mismatch in ticket size vs headline article, but failed to then re‑ground on the coherence of the pattern with a smaller or ancillary art/auction account, and thus over‑penalised.

---

[Rule 13]: When qualitative sources indicate strong control frameworks, treat them as genuine mitigants if no adverse facts are present.  
Applies when:  
- Articles explicitly mention external audits (Big Four, law society, trust‑account audits), government contracts with compliance checks, regulator‑mandated audits, long‑standing clean regulatory records, or verified digital audit trails,  
- There is no direct allegation of ML/TF misuse or control failures for the client, and  
- Quantitative anomalies are modest and structurally explainable.  
Rationale:  
- Many correct trap cases highlighted strict trust‑account rules, daily reconciliations, ASIC/ACNC oversight, government contract vetting, automated ticketing systems, or invoicing that matched bank records.  
- These are not decorative details; they materially reduce the probability that observed anomalies are due to deliberate laundering, especially where sector‑level risk exists.  
- In the auction‑house misclassification, the presence of trust accounts and ASIC‑compliant terms were acknowledged but not given enough downward weight; such governance cues should push risk down when unaccompanied by any contrary evidence.

---

[Rule 14]: Distinguish between “entity‑specific issue under review” and generic “sector risk” when governance concerns arise, and escalate only in the former.  
Applies when:  
- Media mention a specific board conflict, related‑party payment, wage issue, or governance question for the client, and  
- Regulators are “looking into” or “monitoring” but no enforcement action or serious financial crime allegation exists.  
Rationale:  
- Some charity cases involved related‑party subcontracting or governance reviews with ACNC “routine monitoring.” The agent appropriately raised risk slightly but not to high, recognising this is not yet an ML allegation.  
- This pattern contrasts with sleeper/intel cases where regulators explicitly alleged financial facilitation of crime – those warranted high scores.  
- Future reasoning should calibrate: early‑stage governance noise (without misappropriation, fraud, or crime language) warrants at most a minor upward adjustment from low and not a move into high.

---

[Rule 15]: For personal or small‑business accounts with many counterparties but no business explanation, treat elevated fan-out/fan-in more seriously than for well‑documented corporates.  
Applies when:  
- The account is clearly personal or very small‑scale, no payroll/charity/large‑business explanation is present, and  
- Fan‑out/fan‑in are significantly above what’s normal for personal use (e.g., 20+ counterparties), and  
- Adverse media suggests possible facilitation roles (trafficking, bribery, sanctions, hawala, ransomware, tax evasion).  
Rationale:  
- In all sleeper cases involving individuals, modest but broad patterns of counterparties were treated as supportive of the facilitation hypothesis (e.g., 24–33 recipients for persons under investigation), even though they didn’t cross corporate thresholds.  
- By contrast, the same raw numbers were benign in charity or payroll contexts because of the business‑model alibi.  
- The rule: thresholds are relative – for a retiree or sole individual, 25–30 counterparties is a stronger anomaly and should be weighed more heavily if supported by adverse intel.

---

These rules collectively push the agent to:

- Heavily prioritise high‑quality adverse intelligence over “clean” metrics when present.  
- Aggressively down‑weight single, well‑explained structural flags in regulated or transparently legitimate business models.  
- Avoid over‑using generic sector risk or governance noise to justify medium/high ratings in otherwise clean, explained cases (as happened in the auction‑house false positive).
